Belgian dividend tax, rules in plain language

1. The two layers

A foreign dividend hitting an IBKR account passes through two tax layers before it ends up in your hand:

  • Foreign withholding tax, taken at source by the issuer's country. For US dividends this is {{ "%.0f"|format(us_treaty_rate_pct) }}% if you've filed a W-8BEN with IBKR (treaty rate). If no W-8BEN was filed, the US withholds the default 30%, and you can reclaim the 15% difference directly with the IRS.
  • Belgian "précompte mobilier" at {{ "%.0f"|format(belgian_rate_pct) }}%. The base is the dividend net of foreign WHT (rule introduced post-2017). IBKR Ireland does NOT withhold this for you, so you must self-declare and pay.

2. The annual exemption

Belgium exempts the first €{{ "{:,.0f}".format(latest_cap) }} of ordinary dividends per taxpayer per year. Mechanism for foreign dividends: you simply do not declare the first €{{ "{:,.0f}".format(latest_cap) }} of foreign dividends in your tax return. The corresponding foreign WHT also drops out of the calculation (it doesn't get reclaimed but it isn't double-counted either).

3. What's eligible

  • ✅ Ordinary Belgian and foreign dividends (individual stocks)
  • ✅ Dividends from approved cooperatives
  • ✅ Dividends from social-purpose entities
  • ❌ Distributions from collective investment schemes (UCITS, ETFs)
  • ❌ Distributions from common investment funds
  • ❌ Dividends through "legal constructions"
⚠ This calculator currently treats every "Ordinary Dividend" / "Bonus Dividend" row as eligible. If any of your holdings are ETFs or UCITS, the exemption does not apply to those distributions, so you (or your accountant) need to manually subtract them. We can't tell from IBKR's per-payment data alone which symbols are individual stocks vs ETFs.

5. References

  • SPF Finances, Exonération des dividendes (official source, French): fin.belgium.be/fr/particuliers/avantages-fiscaux/exoneration-dividendes
  • BE-US tax treaty (in force since 2007): Belgian treaty index, search "USA"
  • W-8BEN (the form you file with IBKR to claim the 15% treaty rate): irs.gov/forms-pubs/about-form-w-8-ben
  • Belgian tax code, the dividend exemption sits in Code des impôts sur les revenus 1992 (CIR 92), articles 21, 5°-14°. The post-2017 net-base rule comes from changes to article 313 CIR 92.

6. Capital events automatically excluded

IBKR uses the same "Cash Dividend" wrapper for several payouts that are not actually dividend income. Phoenix detects them by the trailing tag in the description (e.g. (InterimLiquidation)) and drops them from this report. They belong to the cost-basis / CGT side of the engine, where they're already captured as a realised gain or loss on the trade.

Excluded tags:

  • InterimLiquidation: bankruptcy estate distributing cash to former shareholders. Example: RGTPQ after the issuer's Chapter 11. The cash partially offsets the cost basis of the (now worthless) shares.
  • LiquidationDividend: final liquidation payout when an entity is wound up.
  • Return of Capital: partial return of original investment (REITs, MLPs). Reduces the cost basis instead of being taxed as ordinary income.
  • Capital Gains Distribution: mutual-fund cap-gains pass-through. Different tax treatment under Belgian rules (taxed only when the fund unit itself is sold).

The match is case-insensitive and runs against both the parsed type tag and the raw description, so unusually-formatted rows are still caught. A matching Withholding Tax row (if any) is excluded too, so the pair vanishes cleanly and doesn't distort the WHT total.

If you need to audit which rows were dropped, search the raw IBKR file for the tags above. They will not appear in the Per payment tab here.

7. Things this report does not do

  • Detect ETF / UCITS distributions automatically (manual exclusion required).
  • Compute the marginal-rate treatment for "Payment in Lieu of Dividend" (we mark these but skip the exemption math).
  • Handle non-US source-country treaty rates (e.g. Brazilian 15% on dividends). We just record what IBKR withheld.
  • Generate the Belgian tax form codes (1444 / 2444 / 1437 etc.). Your accountant does that from this data.
