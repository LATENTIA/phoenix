Belgian corporate income tax, how this report computes it:
  • Flat rate: {{ cit_rate_pct }}%. Applied to every line of investment income; the company's other taxable items (operating profit, salaries, depreciations, prior-year losses) sit outside this report.
  • Realised capital gains and losses on stocks (and on crypto, which Belgian CIT treats as ordinary income) BOTH feed the CIT base. Losses are fully deductible against any corporate income, unlike for individuals.
  • Dividends enter the base at the GROSS amount. The foreign WHT shown on the broker statement is NOT a deduction; it's a credit (FTC) against the Belgian CIT owed on those same dividends.
  • FTC cap: the credit is capped per dividend at the Belgian CIT that would otherwise be due on that dividend ({{ cit_rate_pct }}% × gross). If you paid 15% US WHT, you get 15% back; if you paid 30% (e.g. no W-8BEN), only 25% offsets, the rest is lost.
  • Participation exemption (100% exempt) NOT applied. Belgian CIT exempts dividends and capital gains on shares when the company holds ≥10% of the issuer OR ≥EUR 2.5M acquisition value, held ≥1 year, plus the issuer must be properly taxed. A typical IBKR portfolio of small positions never qualifies, so this report assumes none of them do. If you DO hold a strategic stake, flag it with your accountant.
  • Unrealised gains are ignored. Belgian corporate accounts use historical-cost accounting; mark-to-market only triggers an impairment loss, which Phoenix does not (yet) model.
  • EUR amounts use the ECB EUR/USD reference rate on each event date.
Source: PwC Worldwide Tax Summaries, Belgium / Corporate / Income determination. Phoenix is an audit-trail tool, not a tax-advice tool. Hand these numbers to your accountant and let them apply the rules that depend on info Phoenix can't see (prior-year losses, group taxation, exemption thresholds at the entity level).
